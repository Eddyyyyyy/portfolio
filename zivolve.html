Ziggurat Internship

  • Interaction Designer
  • Internship (mobile app)
  • NDA

Overview

During the summer, I interned at Ziggurat to work on their first product: Zivolve. Zivolve is a mobile investment application that uses AI to help investors make informed decisions. Throughout the internship, my co-intern and I created over 150 high-fidelity screens.

www.zivolve.com

Tools

Figma

Duration

May - Jul 2022 (3 Months)

Why Zivolve?

Hedge funds get rich, while 95% of market traders lose $$$.

In contrast to giant hedge funds, the average market trader does not have the experience or the technology needed to make decisions. Because of this, the playing field heavily favors hedge funds and corporations. Zivolve’s goal is to level the playing field by using AI to suggest decisions based on market behavior and trends.

Responsibilities

Market research -> UI design -> User Flow -> Iterations.

As an interaction design intern, I was to analyze well-established stock applications such as Robinhood, Webull, and Ameritrade. Next, I took the relevant functionality and put my twist that enhanced the UX and made the UI consistent with the current Zivolve app.

Then, I created user flows to communicate my ideas to the CEO and developers through daily standups and weekly meetings. After some consideration and dialogue, I made iterations to the flow and UI.

Impact

I laid the groundwork for most of the features to come.

During my time at Ziggurat, my co-intern and I designed many features. Most screens are still in the agile development process and will be out in the upcoming five product versions. Many of the features I created are essential to the product’s success.

Reflections

I learned to design and simplify the complexities of trading.

Coming into the internship, I knew next to nothing about asset trading. I had to learn how everything worked by talking to the CEO and through my research. I needed to understand trading because the product’s functionality had to be intuitive. The displayed data (such as the various charts, graphs, and tables) needed to make sense to the average joe.
